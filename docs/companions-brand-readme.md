# Companions of CPAS Brand README

Version: v1
Brand purpose: Give Companions of CPAS a clear identity, voice, story system, website copy direction, CMS content rules, and dashboard guidance.

This document focuses less on colors and more on the heart of the organization: who they are, how they help, and how the website should represent them to the internet.

## 1. Brand Core

### Organization

Companions of CPAS

### Full Identity

Companions of CPAS is a volunteer-powered nonprofit supporting dogs at Caddo Parish Animal Services through medical care, transport support, rescue networking, foster placement, adoption visibility, and community action.

### Core Brand Idea

Every dog deserves someone in their corner.

### Mission Statement

Companions of CPAS helps dogs at Caddo Parish Animal Services move from uncertainty toward care, safety, rescue, adoption, and second chances.

### Plain-English Version

They step in for dogs who need more visibility, more care, more time, or a path out.

### What They Really Do

Companions of CPAS is not just a pet adoption organization.

They help create movement for dogs who might otherwise be stuck, overlooked, medically at risk, waiting for transport, needing a foster, or searching for a family.

Their work sits between:

- Shelter support
- Rescue networking
- Foster coordination
- Adoption promotion
- Medical funding
- Transport help
- Community awareness

### Emotional Promise

When someone lands on the site, they should feel:

- These dogs are seen.
- This organization is real.
- My help would actually matter.
- I know exactly what to do next.

## 2. Brand Personality

### The Brand Should Feel

- Warm
- Trustworthy
- Volunteer-powered
- Local
- Compassionate
- Action-oriented
- Hopeful
- Honest
- Protective
- Clear
- Human
- Dog-first

### The Brand Should Not Feel

- Corporate
- Cold
- Generic
- Guilt-heavy
- Chaotic
- Overly cute
- Over-designed
- Like a government form
- Like a wall of Facebook posts
- Like a sad commercial
- Like a complicated shelter database

### Best Description

Companions of CPAS should feel like a trusted neighbor who knows the dogs, knows the need, and knows exactly how the community can help.

## 3. Brand Positioning

### Positioning Statement

Companions of CPAS helps Caddo Parish dogs get the second chance they might not get otherwise by funding care, opening transport pathways, supporting fosters, and connecting dogs with rescue partners and adoptive families.

### Short Positioning Statement

Volunteer-powered second chances for Caddo dogs.

### Homepage-Ready Positioning

Companions of CPAS helps dogs at Caddo Parish Animal Services receive medical care, transport support, foster placement, adoption visibility, and a safer path forward.

### The Difference

Many organizations say adopt, foster, donate.

Companions needs to show what those actions actually do:

- Fostering gives a dog safety and time.
- Transport gives a dog a path to rescue or adoption.
- Donations help pay for medical care and urgent needs.
- Sharing gives a dog visibility.
- Rescue partnerships create outcomes.

The website should connect every CTA to a real outcome.

## 4. Voice & Tone

### Voice Traits

The voice should be:

- Warm but not overly sentimental
- Urgent when needed but not panicked
- Clear but not clinical
- Hopeful but honest
- Specific but easy to read
- Community-centered but dog-first

### Writing Rules

Use:

- Short paragraphs
- Clear headlines
- Specific action language
- Real details when available
- Plain English
- Warm descriptions of each dog
- Outcome-based donation/foster language

Avoid:

- Guilt-heavy language
- Overly long Facebook-style posts on main pages
- Generic nonprofit filler
- Too many exclamation points
- Too many decorative phrases
- Vague CTAs like Learn More when a stronger CTA exists
- Making every dog sound the same

### Website vs Facebook Tone

Facebook can be more emotional, frequent, and story-heavy.

The website should be more curated, calm, and action-focused.

The website should not copy Facebook post structure directly. It should translate the best Facebook content into clear website modules.

## 5. Copy Pillars

### Pillar 1: Second Chances

This is the emotional center of the brand.

Use when talking about the mission, homepage, about page, donations, and rescue outcomes.

Example lines:

- Second chances start when someone says yes.
- We help Caddo dogs reach the care, rescue, and families waiting for them.
- Some dogs need more than a kennel card. They need a path forward.

### Pillar 2: Someone in Their Corner

This is the protective, human side of the brand.

Use when talking about Companions' role.

Example lines:

- Every dog deserves someone in their corner.
- We help dogs who need extra visibility, care, or time.
- When a dog needs help getting to the next step, Companions works to open the door.

### Pillar 3: Volunteer-Powered

This builds trust and explains the organization.

Use when talking about donations, operations, and community.

Example lines:

- Companions is volunteer-powered and community-supported.
- Every gift helps move dogs closer to care, transport, rescue, or home.
- The work happens because people choose to step in.

### Pillar 4: Practical Help

This keeps the brand grounded.

Use when talking about fostering, transport, medical care, and rescue.

Example lines:

- A short-term foster can give a dog the time they need to reach transport.
- Medical gifts help cover care for dogs who arrive injured, sick, or overlooked.
- Transport support helps dogs reach rescue partners and approved homes.

## 6. Primary Audiences

### Local Adopters

They need to quickly see adoptable dogs, understand the basics, and know who to contact.

They care about:

- Personality
- Age
- Size
- Compatibility
- Readiness
- Adoption contact

### Fosters

They may be nervous or unsure.

They need reassurance:

- How long is fostering?
- Are supplies provided?
- What support is available?
- What kind of dogs need fosters?
- Who do they contact?

### Donors

They need trust and clarity.

They care about:

- Where money goes
- What needs are urgent
- Whether the organization is legitimate
- Whether small donations matter

### Rescue Partners

They need clear dog details and contact paths.

They care about:

- Status
- Medical notes
- Transport timing
- Rescue contact
- Photos/details
- Urgency

### Community Supporters

They may not adopt or foster but will share, donate, volunteer, or follow.

They need:

- Stories
- Wins
- Easy ways to help
- Clear proof of impact

## 8. Content System

### Animal Feature Format

Use this structure for adoptable dogs:

Title:

Meet [Dog Name]

Short intro:

[Dog Name] is a [age] [breed/mix] looking for [adoption/foster/rescue/transport support].

Personality:

Describe the dog warmly and specifically.

Quick facts:

- Age
- Weight
- Sex/fixed status
- Animal ID
- Medical notes when appropriate
- Compatibility notes when known

CTA:

Contact about [Dog Name]

### Foster Needed Format

Title:

Foster Needed for [Dog Name]

Body:

[Dog Name] needs a temporary foster home while they wait for [transport/rescue/adoption/medical recovery].

Quick facts:

- Age
- Foster duration if known
- Transport date if known
- Supplies/support note
- Contact person

CTA:

Contact About Fostering

### Feel-Good Story Format

Title:

[Dog Name] Got Their Next Chapter

Body:

Tell the story briefly. Focus on the moment someone stepped in, what changed, and what the outcome was.

CTA:

Help Create the Next Second Chance

### Medical Need Format

Title:

Help [Dog Name] Get the Care They Need

Body:

Explain the need respectfully. Make the donation purpose specific without making the story feel exploitative.

CTA:

Donate Toward Medical Care

## 9. Design Identity Guidance

### Current Theme Direction

The current theme is worth improving, not throwing away.

Strengths:

- Clean modern type
- Strong dark/light contrast between sections
- Purple accent is memorable
- Large emotional dog imagery works
- Rounded cards feel friendly
- Dashboard has a strong foundation

Improvements needed:

- Better color contrast in dashboard text
- Less duplicated copy
- Cleaner hero layout on About
- More warmth in public page copy
- Stronger brand identity beyond generic rescue language
- More consistent CTA hierarchy
- Better mobile spacing and tap targets
- Better use of full-screen dashboard layouts

### Typography Direction

The current visual direction reads like a modern Inter / Geist / SF Pro-style sans system.

Use this lane unless the live CSS confirms a different font stack:

- Strong 800/900 headings
- Clean 400/500 body copy
- Modern sans UI labels
- Minimal decorative type

Do not bring Facebook flyer-style script fonts into the primary website UI. If decorative type is ever used, keep it rare and accent-only.

### Visual Personality

The website should feel like:

- Modern nonprofit
- Warm local rescue
- Clean digital action system
- Emotional but organized
- Trustworthy but not sterile

The design should not feel like:

- A government shelter portal
- A generic animal rescue template
- A cluttered Facebook feed
- A flyer pasted onto a webpage

## 10. Dashboard Brand Rules

The dashboard should help the team do real work without feeling technical.

### Dashboard Promise

The dashboard should make it easy to keep dogs visible, update their status, manage media, review inquiries, and publish website changes.

### Dashboard Language

Use plain labels:

- Add Animal
- Update Status
- Mark Foster Needed
- Add Photo
- Publish Page
- Save Draft
- View Inquiries
- Review Applications

Avoid overly technical labels when a rescue user would not naturally say them.

### Dashboard Priorities

The dashboard should support:

- Animal records
- Foster needs
- Adoption/contact flow
- Media management
- Page editing
- Donation/fundraising content
- Reports
- Settings/integrations
- Email/inquiries

### Dashboard Boundary

Do not silently include unlimited AI, storage, email, or social posting usage.

Client-owned integrations should be used for:

- Cloudflare resources
- R2 media storage
- Meta/Facebook publishing
- Resend email
- AI/API usage
- Any automated posting or content generation

## 11. Brand Do / Don't

### Do

- Lead with the dog.
- Make every next step obvious.
- Use warm, human copy.
- Show real outcomes.
- Explain why fostering matters.
- Explain what donations fund.
- Keep adoption/rescue contacts clear.
- Use candid photos when they show personality.
- Use curated stories instead of dumping full social feeds.
- Keep the website calmer than Facebook.

### Don't

- Make every page feel urgent.
- Use guilt as the main motivator.
- Overload pages with long paragraphs.
- Hide contact information.
- Use vague CTAs.
- Overuse decorative fonts.
- Let dashboard demo data look real.
- Let AI/social automation create surprise costs.
- Make the website harder to maintain than the Facebook page.

## 12. Final Brand Summary

Companions of CPAS is a volunteer-powered rescue support organization helping Caddo dogs receive the care, visibility, transport, foster support, rescue connections, and adoption opportunities they need.

The public website should make the work easy to understand and easy to support.

The dashboard should make the work easier to manage.

The brand should feel compassionate, clear, hopeful, and grounded in real outcomes.

Core message:

Every dog deserves someone in their corner.
